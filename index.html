    Ocean pollution in the worlds rivers

    Read More

    As many people know, the amount of ocean pollution is rising. Whether it is plastics, oil, or polystyrene, there is an ever-rising amount of pollution in the world's oceans. With the help of new measurements and modelling, new research shows that about 1000 rivers emit 80% of all plastic emissions into the world's oceans.

    The belief is that since there is such a small quantity of rivers, these rivers are the major contributors to ocean pollution. Every minute, about 2500 kilograms of plastic enters the ocean. That means that 3.6 million kilograms of plastic enter the ocean each day, or 3600 tons of plastic each day.

    Over the course of a year, around 8 million tons of plastic will enter the oceans. Every day, rivers carry not just sediments and water, but also huge amounts of plastic into the oceans. On earth, there are more than 100,000 rivers, but only 1000 of them contribute 80% of the world’s ocean pollution.

    One Percent of the rivers worldwide, represent one of the largest contributors of ocean plastics in the world

    While this seems like many rivers, this number is only 1% of all the rivers conducted in this study. This means that a targeted approach to ocean clean-up could see a drastic change in the world’s ocean pollution.

    The image above shows these rivers, and more. The orange/red dots represent the 100 rivers, mentioned previously, and the blue dots represent the remaining rivers which contribute to only 20% of pollution, even though there is more of them than the larger, and more polluted rivers, represented by the blue orange/red dots.

    China's contribution to ocean pollution

    Read More

    It makes sense that China is one of the largest ocean polluters in the world, as they are the biggest producer and exporter of plastic products, which accounts for about 30% of the world’s total. However, China is attempting to combat this rising issue. China has recently allocated 7 billion yuan, which is equivalent to 1.5 billion Australian dollars, to attempting to solve and fix the rising issue of plastics and other kinds of waste in our oceans.

    Although people in China generally generate less plastic waste per capita than other people from other countries such as America and Germany, almost three quarters of China’s plastic waste ends up in poorly managed landfills or out in the open.

    Recently, China has also announced that it will ban all plastics which pollute its lands and the world’s oceans. However, there has been some controversy over this, as it would be extremely hard to regulate and control all the plastic pollution within China, as it is such a large land mass, and there is so much of this plastic circulating through China’s factories, and exporters/importers.

    China dumped a total of 200.7 million cubic meters of waste into its coastal waters in 2018, which was a 27% rise on the previous year and has been the highest level in at least a decade.

    However, officials in China have a plan, which involves a regulatory policy which would ensure that factories adhere to responsible waste management, and ultimately to reduce the amount of pollution by China into the oceans. The Chinese government stated that the companies and consumers within China need to get used to life with much less single-use plastics.

    These new pollution policies will begin in larger cities such as Beijing and Shanghai, and then eventually move to smaller cities and towns, and finally to small villages. By the end of 2025, China predicts that their plastic pollution finding its way into the ocean will have been drastically reduced.

    Oil pollution and spills

    Read More

    A major contributor to ocean pollution is oil. Oil makes its way into the ocean from shipping oil spills, drains, and dumping of oil. When an oil spill occurs from an oil tanker, it causes an extreme problem, as these oil tankers are carrying very large amounts of oil, which means that as the oil spill occurs, it concentrates the pollution in the waters around it.

    Oil cannot dissolve in water, and it forms a thick sludge in the water. This suffocates fish, and gets caught in the feathers of birds, which stops them from being able to fly. The oil also affects the marine aquatic plants, who rely on photosynthesis to be able to survive as the oil blocks the light from reaching them.

    12% of the oil in the world's oceans comes from just oil spills. This means that the other 88% is from drains, sewage systems, and dumping of oil waste. On the 20th of April 2010, the largest oil spill in history occurred off the coast of the Gulf of Mexico.

    Over the course of 10 minutes, 1000 litres of oil can spread a radius of 5 metres from the origin of the spill and create a sludge 10 cm thick. Since oil spills aren't often contained or dealt with extremely fast, it can often be very hard to control and slow down the rate of spread.

    The oil drilling rig, Deepwater Horizon, which was operating in the Macondo Prospect in the Gulf of Mexico, exploded, and unfortunately sank, which resulted in the death of 11 workers, and the largest oil spill in the history of maritime oil operations.

    Over 4 million barrels of oil flowed from the rig over the 87-day period, before the spill was controlled. Overall, oil spills can be extremely harmful to the environment, and if not controlled properly or soon enough, possibly catastrophic for the environment.

    Run-off into the oceans

    Read More

    Run-off occurs when there is more water than the land below can absorb. This excess liquid will flow across the surface of the land below, and it will make its way into nearby streams, creeks, ponds, or any other body of water. Run-off can cause many issues to the surrounding environment.

    For example, it can deteriorate plants, buildings, and the surrounding terrain. Along its path, it can carry pollutants which may lie in the soil, and surrounding terrain. As the liquid moves, it carries these pollutants with it. Along its journey, it can pick up enormous quantities of pollutants. This means that when it encounters a body of water, it will discharge the waste into it. This can cause serious issues.

    A local example of where run-off is causing ocean pollution, is inshore areas of Australia's Great Barrier Reef. Run-off is a major contributor to pollution in these bodies of water. There are 35 catchments which flow into the Great Barrier Reef. Grazing lands are the main contributor of fine sediment, and particulate nitrogen.

    Entire ecosystems can be disrupted when our river systems transport unwanted nutrients and sediments into our oceans.

    This means as liquid travels through these lands, it picks up, and takes these sediments to the reef. These sediments are deposited on the reef, which in turn damages the coral. Sedimentation damages coral, because it blocks the coral's ability to feed, and absorb light. This results in coral bleaching, which is the result of coral dying from limited sources of food and light.

    Nutrients can also encourage excess algae, which competes against the coral for the space within the reef. Epiphytic algae can reduce the capacity of seagrasses to photosynthesise. In conclusion, run-off can cause significant impacts on the marine environment, often many kilometres away from the actual source of pollution.
